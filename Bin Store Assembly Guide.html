Tools Required

Before you start, gather these essential tools. Most are standard for woodworking projects.

Cutting & Measuring

Handsaw or Circular Saw
For cutting timber to length. Circular saw is faster; handsaw works but more tiring.
Tape Measure (5m minimum)
Essential for accurate dimensions. Marks every 50mm helps with layout.
Combination Square or Framing Square
Ensures 90° corners and helps mark cut lines perpendicular to timber.
Spirit Level (1.2m preferred)
Check vertical and horizontal alignment at each stage. Essential for doors to hang straight.

Assembly & Fastening

Cordless Drill / Driver
For drilling pilot holes and driving screws. 18V minimum recommended.
Countersink Drill Bit
Creates conical holes for screw heads to sit flush in cladding.
Impact Driver (optional but useful)
Faster for driving large screws into frame joints.
Claw Hammer
For nailing roof felt and shingles. Roofing nail puller helps if mistakes happen.

Safety & Finishing

Safety Glasses & Gloves
Protect eyes from sawdust and splinters. Pressure-treated timber is non-toxic but still an irritant.
Work Gloves
Prevent splinters when handling rough-sawn timber.
Paint Roller & Brush (25–50mm)
For applying wood paint and paint.
Orbital Sander (optional)
Smooth rough edges before varnishing. Orbital sanders are gentler than belt sanders.

Miscellaneous

Clamps (2–4 required)
Hold frames square while fastening. Bar clamps or pipe clamps work well.
Workbench or Sawhorse
Stable surface for cutting and assembly. Two sawhorses are practical for outdoor work.
Ladder (ideally 3–4 steps)
For fitting roof panels and shingles at height. Must reach 1600mm.
Rubber Mallet
Tap featheredge boards into place without damage.

Phased Weekend Build Plan

This bin store can be built over four separate weekends, with the priority of getting a weatherproof structure standing after Weekend 2. The phased approach lets you spread the work and tackle different tasks each session without rushing.

Why Phase the Build? Weekend 1 establishes the solid frame skeleton. The frame can be left exposed for a week or two (cover it with a tarp or sheet if rain is forecast). Weekend 2 completes the roof to make the structure fully weatherproof. Once the frame and roof are complete, you can leave the structure unattended for weeks while you gather materials and energy for cladding, shelving, and finishing. This approach is ideal for DIY builders working part-time or managing tool/material logistics.

Weekend 1: Frame

Objective: Build the structural frame skeleton. The frame will be the foundation for all future work. You can drape a tarp or sheet over the top if rain is expected before Weekend 2 when the roof goes on.

Materials Needed This Weekend

Structural Timber:
  • 10 × Snowdon Treated Pine 50×47mm, 2.4m (uprights & rails)
  • 8 × Rough Sawn Treated Whitewood 50×47mm, 1.8m (cross-bracing & horizontal rails)
  • 4 × Rough Sawn Treated Whitewood 50×22mm, 1.8m (roof battens)
Fixings:
  • Galvanised Screws (40mm, 50mm, 75mm sizes) — ~60–80 pieces

Tools Needed This Weekend

Essential:
  • Handsaw or Circular Saw (for cutting timber)
  • Tape Measure (5m minimum)
  • Combination Square or Framing Square
  • Spirit Level (1.2m preferred)
  • Cordless Drill / Driver (18V minimum)
  • Clamps (2–4, for holding frames square)
  • Sawhorses or Workbench
Safety:
  • Safety Glasses
  • Work Gloves
  • Dust Mask (when cutting treated timber)

What You'll Have at the End of Weekend 1

A solid frame skeleton with:
  • All 6 uprights installed (2 back at 1600mm, 2 front at 1550mm, 2 centre at 1550mm)
  • Top and bottom rails connecting all uprights
  • Centre divider creating the left/right sections
  • Mid-height horizontal rails for cladding support
The frame structure is complete and can be left as-is for 1–2 weeks before the roof goes on in Weekend 2. We recommend draping a tarp or sheet over the top if rain is forecast during this period.
1 Cut All Timber to Size

Using the cut list above, measure and cut every piece of timber before assembly. This saves time and ensures accuracy. For this weekend, we only need the frame timber.

Cutting Guide:
  • Frame posts & rails: Use a circular saw or handsaw. Cut all 2.4m sticks to uprights first (1600mm, 1550mm), then rails & braces.
  • Roof battens: These will be cut to length (approx 1300mm) and laid horizontally on top of the frame, but you can prepare them now if you prefer.
⚠ Measure twice, cut once! Pressure-treated timber is expensive. Double-check dimensions before cutting.

2 Assemble the Back Frame

The back frame is 1300mm wide and 1600mm tall. This is the tallest, most structural part. Build it flat on sawhorses.

Materials needed:
  • 2× back corner posts (1600mm, 50×47mm)
  • 1× back top rail (1300mm, 50×47mm)
  • 1× back bottom rail (1300mm, 50×47mm)
  • 1× back mid-height rail (1300mm, 50×47mm) at ~775mm from bottom
  • 2× diagonal braces (~1050mm at 45°, 50×47mm)
Fixings (use stainless/galvanised):
  • 2× 75mm screws per corner joint (corner to post)
  • 2× 75mm screws per horizontal rail connection
  • 2× 65mm screws per brace end
  • Aim for at least 2 fasteners per joint, more for load-bearing connections.

Assembly method:

  1. Lay the two back posts flat on sawhorses, 1300mm apart (measured corner-to-corner).
  2. Attach the top rail between them using 75mm screws — 2 per corner, driven through the rail into the post ends. Drill pilot holes first to prevent splitting.
  3. Attach the bottom rail the same way, ensuring it's square with the posts using a framing square.
  4. Add the mid-height rail at ~775mm from the bottom, attaching with 2 screws per end.
  5. Install X-braces diagonally (from top-left to bottom-right and vice versa). Angle the ends at 45° so they sit flush on the posts. Use 2 screws per brace end.
  6. Check diagonal measurements (corner-to-corner): both should be equal, confirming the frame is square. Clamp & adjust if needed.
Pro tip: Use a spirit level on the posts to ensure they're vertical (perpendicular to sawhorses). A slight tilt now will compound through the build.

4 Connect Front to Back with Depth Rails

Now join the front and back frames with horizontal rails running left-right along the sides. This creates the full 3D structure.

Materials needed:
  • 4× depth rails (750mm, 50×47mm) — two at top, two at bottom
  • One pair each on left & right sides
Fixings:
  • 2× 75mm screws per depth rail end (into the corner posts of front & back frames)

Assembly:

  1. Position the back frame vertically, secured by props or clamps so it won't topple.
  2. Position the front frame vertically, facing the back, 750mm away (the depth of the bin store).
  3. Attach a top-left depth rail between the top-left corner posts, using 75mm screws. Drive screws through the rail into each post.
  4. Attach a bottom-left depth rail the same way at the base.
  5. Repeat on the right side with top-right and bottom-right depth rails.
  6. Check that the structure is square using a spirit level on all corners & a tape measure on diagonals (both diagonals should be equal).
  7. Brace the entire structure with temporary props so it won't shift while you add the centre divider.
Safety: A 1300×750×1600mm wooden structure is heavy and unstable until fully braced. Do NOT work on this alone. Use clamps, props, or a helper to prevent it tipping. Once the centre divider & cladding are in place, it will be much more rigid.

5 Install the Centre Divider

The centre divider is a full-height 50×47mm post running front-to-back at the midline. It separates the enclosed left section from the open right section and carries significant load from the roof.

Materials needed:
  • 1× centre divider post (1550mm, 50×47mm) — runs vertical from front to back
  • 1× centre depth rail (750mm, 50×47mm) — connects centre post to back wall at top
  • 1× centre mid-height rail (750mm, 50×47mm) — horizontal support at ~775mm height
Fixings:
  • 3× 75mm screws: 2 at base (into front bottom rail), 1 at top (connection to back frame)
  • 2× 75mm screws per mid-height rail attachment

Assembly:

  1. Position the centre post vertically, centred front-to-back (at 650mm from one front corner, 650mm from the other). This post should be perfectly plumb (vertical).
  2. Attach it at the base by screwing down into the front bottom rail (2 screws, 75mm, driven through the base into the rail).
  3. Attach a depth rail from the top of the centre post to the back frame, screwing 2×75mm screws per end.
  4. Install the mid-height rail (750mm) at ~775mm height, connecting the centre post to other frame members. This provides structural support for shelves & roof loading.
  5. Check the post is perfectly vertical using a spirit level.
Design note: The 750mm depth allows bins to be loaded on the diagonal past the centre post. This is intentional — it gives maximum usable width in the enclosed section.

1 Prepare the Roof Area and Lay Plywood Sheathing

The roof slopes slightly from back (1600mm tall) to front (1550mm tall), creating a gentle pitch. Lay the plywood on top of the roof battens (the 4 × 50×22mm pieces installed horizontally on top of the frame).

Steps:
  1. Position the 18mm exterior plywood sheet on top of the roof battens, ensuring it sits evenly.
  2. Secure the plywood to the battens using 40mm or 50mm galvanised screws (or nails) at approximately 200mm intervals. Use at least 1 fastener per batten.
  3. Ensure the plywood is flush and level. Check there are no gaps between the plywood and the battens.
  4. If the plywood overhangs the edges, trim it flush with a handsaw or circular saw for a clean edge.
⚠ Work safely at height: Use a sturdy ladder (3–4 steps) and have a helper stabilise it. Wear safety glasses and gloves when handling roofing materials.
2 Unroll and Secure the Bitumen Felt Underlay

The bitumen felt provides a secondary water barrier under the shingles and improves roof durability.

Steps:
  1. Unroll the Roof Pro bitumen felt across the roof, starting from the bottom (front edge) and working upwards.
  2. Align the bottom edge flush with the front edge of the plywood. Allow a slight overhang (50–100mm) at the eaves for water runoff.
  3. Secure the felt to the plywood using clout nails (13mm) spaced approximately 150–200mm apart. Drive nails through the felt into the plywood below.
  4. Overlap each roll of felt by at least 100mm (a 10cm overlap) to prevent water penetration. Ensure overlaps are taped or nailed down securely.
  5. Work your way up the roof towards the back, rolling out and nailing felt as you go.
  6. At the top of the roof (back edge), fold or trim the felt for a clean edge.
Tip: Bitumen felt can be slippery when wet or sunny. Wear soft-soled shoes or boots with good grip for safety when working on the roof.
3 Lay and Nail Bitumen Shingles

Bitumen shingles are the final weatherproof layer, protecting the structure from rain and UV damage.

Steps:
  1. Start at the bottom (front edge) of the roof. Lay the first row of shingles along the bottom edge, aligning them straight and leaving no gaps.
  2. Each shingle should overlap the bottom edge by approximately 50mm (half the shingle width).
  3. Secure each shingle with 4 clout nails: 1 nail at each tab edge (approximately 25mm from the shingle edges) and 2 nails in the middle of the shingle. Space nails evenly and drive them straight in.
  4. Lay the second row of shingles above the first, overlapping the first row by approximately 150mm (ensuring water runs downward). Stagger the shingles so the tabs of the upper row are offset from the tabs of the lower row (this is called a 'staggered bond').
  5. Continue laying rows until you reach the top (back edge) of the roof.
  6. At the top, fold or trim the final row of shingles for a neat finish. Secure with nails or a dab of roofing adhesive if needed.
⚠ Nail placement is critical: Nails must go through the marked nail line (usually a tab or slot) on each shingle. Improper nailing can cause shingles to lift in wind or trap water underneath.

2 Assemble the Back Frame

The back frame is 1300mm wide and 1600mm tall. This is the tallest, most structural part. Build it flat on sawhorses.

Materials needed:
  • 2× back corner posts (1600mm, 50×47mm)
  • 1× back top rail (1300mm, 50×47mm)
  • 1× back bottom rail (1300mm, 50×47mm)
  • 1× back mid-height rail (1300mm, 50×47mm) at ~775mm from bottom
  • 2× diagonal braces (~1050mm at 45°, 50×47mm)
Fixings (use stainless/galvanised):
  • 2× 75mm screws per corner joint (corner to post)
  • 2× 75mm screws per horizontal rail connection
  • 2× 65mm screws per brace end
  • Aim for at least 2 fasteners per joint, more for load-bearing connections.

Assembly method:

  1. Lay the two back posts flat on sawhorses, 1300mm apart (measured corner-to-corner).
  2. Attach the top rail between them using 75mm screws — 2 per corner, driven through the rail into the post ends. Drill pilot holes first to prevent splitting.
  3. Attach the bottom rail the same way, ensuring it's square with the posts using a framing square.
  4. Add the mid-height rail at ~775mm from the bottom, attaching with 2 screws per end.
  5. Install X-braces diagonally (from top-left to bottom-right and vice versa). Angle the ends at 45° so they sit flush on the posts. Use 2 screws per brace end.
  6. Check diagonal measurements (corner-to-corner): both should be equal, confirming the frame is square. Clamp & adjust if needed.
Pro tip: Use a spirit level on the posts to ensure they're vertical (perpendicular to sawhorses). A slight tilt now will compound through the build.
3 Assemble the Front Frame

The front frame is 1300mm wide and 1550mm tall (50mm lower than the back). Build it the same way as the back, then stand both frames upright.

Materials needed:
  • 2× front corner posts (1550mm, 50×47mm)
  • 1× front top rail (1300mm, 50×47mm)
  • 1× front bottom rail (1300mm, 50×47mm)
  • 2× diagonal braces (~1000mm at 45°, 50×47mm)

Assembly: Follow the same method as Step 2, but posts are 50mm shorter. When both back & front frames are complete, stand them upright next to each other to check alignment. They should look like a → ╴ shape in profile, with the back taller.

4 Connect Front to Back with Depth Rails

Now join the front and back frames with horizontal rails running left-right along the sides. This creates the full 3D structure.

Materials needed:
  • 4× depth rails (750mm, 50×47mm) — two at top, two at bottom
  • One pair each on left & right sides
Fixings:
  • 2× 75mm screws per depth rail end (into the corner posts of front & back frames)

Assembly:

  1. Position the back frame vertically, secured by props or clamps so it won't topple.
  2. Position the front frame vertically, facing the back, 750mm away (the depth of the bin store).
  3. Attach a top-left depth rail between the top-left corner posts, using 75mm screws. Drive screws through the rail into each post.
  4. Attach a bottom-left depth rail the same way at the base.
  5. Repeat on the right side with top-right and bottom-right depth rails.
  6. Check that the structure is square using a spirit level on all corners & a tape measure on diagonals (both diagonals should be equal).
  7. Brace the entire structure with temporary props so it won't shift while you add the centre divider.
Safety: A 1300×750×1600mm wooden structure is heavy and unstable until fully braced. Do NOT work on this alone. Use clamps, props, or a helper to prevent it tipping. Once the centre divider & cladding are in place, it will be much more rigid.

5 Install the Centre Divider

The centre divider is a full-height 50×47mm post running front-to-back at the midline. It separates the enclosed left section from the open right section and carries significant load from the roof.

Materials needed:
  • 1× centre divider post (1550mm, 50×47mm) — runs vertical from front to back
  • 1× centre depth rail (750mm, 50×47mm) — connects centre post to back wall at top
  • 1× centre mid-height rail (750mm, 50×47mm) — horizontal support at ~775mm height
Fixings:
  • 3× 75mm screws: 2 at base (into front bottom rail), 1 at top (connection to back frame)
  • 2× 75mm screws per mid-height rail attachment

Assembly:

  1. Position the centre post vertically, centred front-to-back (at 650mm from one front corner, 650mm from the other). This post should be perfectly plumb (vertical).
  2. Attach it at the base by screwing down into the front bottom rail (2 screws, 75mm, driven through the base into the rail).
  3. Attach a depth rail from the top of the centre post to the back frame, screwing 2×75mm screws per end.
  4. Install the mid-height rail (750mm) at ~775mm height, connecting the centre post to other frame members. This provides structural support for shelves & roof loading.
  5. Check the post is perfectly vertical using a spirit level.
Design note: The 750mm depth allows bins to be loaded on the diagonal past the centre post. This is intentional — it gives maximum usable width in the enclosed section.

  2. On the right side, install a mid-height rail along the solid front panel (right section only), running from the centre divider to the right-front corner. This supports the right-front featheredge panel.
  3. Check all rails are level using a spirit level.
7 Install Shelf Battens (Right Section Only)

The right section is open-sided with three horizontal shelves for recycling boxes. Install thin support battens at three heights.

Materials needed:
  • 6× shelf battens (750mm, 50×22mm) — two battens per shelf level, three levels
  • 3× shelf boards (plywood, ~700–750mm × 750mm deep) — custom cut from 18mm exterior plywood
Fixings:
  • 2× 65mm screws per batten end (into the posts/dividers)

Assembly:

  1. Decide on shelf heights. A typical spacing for 500–600mm tall recycling boxes is every 500mm vertically. Levels might be at 400mm, 900mm, and 1400mm from the bottom.
  2. On the back wall (right side of the centre divider), attach two parallel 50×22mm battens at the first level (e.g., 400mm height), running front-to-back. Use 2 screws per end into the back frame & centre post.
  3. On the front-right corner post, attach another pair of battens at the same height, running front-to-back, to create a frame for the shelf board.
  4. Repeat for the second level (e.g., 900mm) and third level (e.g., 1400mm).
  5. Cut plywood shelf boards to fit between the battens. They should rest on top of the 50×22mm battens. Use 2–3 screws per board to lock it in place.
Shelf design: Because the right section is open on the side (right-facing), there is no wall to support the shelf outboard. The battens must be sturdy & well-fastened. Recycling boxes will rest on these shelves, adding lateral load.

8 Fit Cladding — Back Wall

Start cladding the structure. The back wall uses featheredge boards (150mm width, 11mm thickness), which overlap to shed water naturally. Work from bottom to top.

DIAGRAM 5: CLADDING BEING APPLIED

Cladding applied in sequence: back, left walls, centre divider left face, and front sections. Right side open for shelving access.

Materials needed:
  • ~13–15 featheredge boards (150mm width, 11mm thickness, 2.4m length)
  • From the 3 packs (30 boards total) in the shopping list
Fixings:
  • 2–3× 40mm stainless/galvanised screws per board (one per post/rail intersection)
  • Countersink screws so they sit flush or slightly below the surface

Assembly:

  1. Start at the bottom. Lay the first featheredge board horizontally, aligned flush with the bottom rail. The board should span 1300mm (the full width of the back).
  2. Cut the board to length if necessary. Featheredge boards overlap (each board overlaps the one below by ~25mm), so the next board above will fit snugly.
  3. Drill pilot holes through the board at each rail & post location to prevent splitting.
  4. Drive 2–3 × 40mm screws per board, fastening it to the frame. Position screws so they don't interfere with the interlocking edge of the next board.
  5. Add the next board above, overlapping the board below by ~25mm. Nail through both layers into the horizontal rail behind. Drive screws into this board.
  6. Repeat until you reach the top. The final board at the top (1600mm height) may need to be cut at an angle to follow the sloped roof line.
  7. The back wall is now weatherproofed & rigid.
⚠ Cladding alignment: Check that each board is level (horizontal) using a spirit level before fastening. Featheredge overlaps rely on gravity to shed water — if a board is tilted, water will pool & cause rot.

13 Apply Roof Underlay (Bitumen Felt)

Unroll the bitumen felt underlay across the plywood deck. This is the primary waterproof membrane. The bitumen shingles sit on top of this layer.

  • 13mm galvanised clout nails, spaced every ~150–200mm around edges & ~400mm in the field

Assembly:

  1. Unroll the felt starting from the bottom (front) of the roof, laying it flat on the plywood.

13 Apply Roof Underlay (Bitumen Felt)

Unroll the bitumen felt underlay across the plywood deck. This is the primary waterproof membrane. The bitumen shingles sit on top of this layer.

Materials needed:

  • Bitumen roof felt nails / clout nails (13mm, from shopping list)
Fixings:
  • 13mm galvanised clout nails, spaced every ~150–200mm around edges & ~400mm in the field

Assembly:

  1. Unroll the felt starting from the bottom (front) of the roof, laying it flat on the plywood.

  4. Continue until the entire roof is covered. The felt should extend past the front edge of the roof to direct water away.
  5. Do NOT pull the felt too tight — it should have slight slack to allow for expansion in heat.
⚠ Felt safety: Bitumen felt can be slippery. Wear non-slip shoes & be careful working at height. A 3–4 step ladder is usually sufficient to reach 1600mm.

Assembly Notes & Tips

Time Estimates

Experienced builder (1 person): 5–6 days (cutting, assembly, cladding, roof, finishing)
Two people: 3–4 days (can work in parallel: one cuts, one builds)
Beginner (1 person): 7–8 days (allow extra time for double-checking & adjustments)

Weather Considerations

  • Avoid building in heavy rain or strong wind — the partially-built frame can be unstable.
  • Pressure-treated timber can absorb moisture. Allow it to dry for 24 hours after sawing before sealing.
  • paint and paint dry slower in cold or humid weather. Plan finishing work for a dry forecast (48+ hours no rain expected).

Tools You Can Hire (if you don't own)

  • Circular saw or circular saw table: £20–40/day. Essential for quick, accurate cuts.
  • Orbital sander: £15–25/day. Speeds up edge finishing before paint.
  • Ladder (3–4 step): £10–15/day. Necessary for roof work.
  • Power drill & driver: Most hardware stores have these as part of budget packages (£50–100 to buy, or £10–20/day to hire).

Safety Reminders

  • Always wear safety glasses when sawing or using power tools.
  • Pressure-treated timber dust can irritate respiratory systems. Wear a dust mask when cutting.
  • Work at heights (roof access) only with proper ladder & stability. Never lean beyond the ladder side rails.
  • Screws & nails have sharp ends. Handle with care & dispose of waste safely.
  • Bitumen felt can be slippery, especially on slopes. Wear appropriate footwear & use caution on the roof.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Not checking square/level at each stage: Small errors compound. Use a spirit level & framing square regularly.
  • Oversizing screw holes: This weakens joints. Drill pilot holes slightly smaller than the screw shank.
  • Skipping the paint coat: Pressure-treated timber is protected internally, but exposed surfaces still benefit from a paint coat.
  • Forcing featheredge boards together: They interlock by design. If a board is hard to slide in, check for obstructions or slight warping. A rubber mallet helps without damage.
  • Installing cladding before the frame is square: Cladding is a surface; it doesn't fix a crooked frame. Always ensure frame squareness first.

Maintenance & Lifespan

  • Year 1–2: Minimal maintenance. Check fasteners annually for tightness, especially hinge bolts on the door.
  • Year 2–3: Reapply paint to any worn or faded areas (especially south-facing cladding exposed to UV).
  • Year 3–5: Consider a full refresh — sand the cladding lightly & apply fresh paint to extend life.
  • Roof inspection: Check shingles & felt annually, especially after storms. Replace damaged shingles as needed.
  • Expected lifespan: With care, 15–20 years for the structure; roof may need refreshing at 10–12 years.
